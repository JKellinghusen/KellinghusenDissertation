Protection from Harm

The first Essential of this Framework is Protection from Harm. This Essential rests on two human needs: safety and security. More than two-in-five workers surveyed by the American Psychological Association in 2022 reported that health and safety concerns negatively affect their stress level at work. - The U.S. Surgeon Generals Framework for Workplace Mental Health & Well-Being

 

Prioritize workplace physical safety

Why it matters

Physical safety is a critical concern for many aid organizations working in hazardous locations such as conflict zones or regions experiencing natural disasters, which expose workers to accidents, injuries, or interpersonal violence. In 2024, 336 major attacks against aid workers were recorded by the Aid Worker Security Database, the deadliest year for aid workers, according to the UN. Aside from acute safety incidents, exposure to traumatic events and chronic stress from living with the risk of harm increases the prevalence of PTSD, depression, anxiety disorder, and burnout among employees. Unsafe work environments and chronic stress have been shown to contribute to reduced productivity and increased absenteeism and turnover.

While dangerous working conditions are often unavoidable in the aid sector, organizations must do their best to maximize the safety of their staff by providing resources and creating supportive policies. Aid organizations have a duty of care to protect their employees not just from external threats, but also from internal problems such as harassment and violence between employees.

Recommendations

Conduct regular safety audits

To address the safety issues affecting your staff, you must first understand what the issues are. If your operations are complex and you have sufficient funds, you may want to contract an external company to complete an official safety audit periodically.

If this approach is not feasible or necessary for your organization, you can apply the same principles to conduct an internal safety audit. Start with a standard workplace safety audit framework and adapt it to your specific needs and circumstances by consulting your team and referring to documentation such as the United Nations Security Management System and the OCHA Safety and Security for National Humanitarian Workers report. Evaluate safety risks internal to the organization, such as harassment and bullying. Set a schedule for completing additional audits every few years or multiple times per year, depending on the security situation in which you work, to adapt to the changing challenges to safety.

Make sure to include your staff in all levels of the organization in the audit process, from planning the audit to making recommendations to address safety concerns. This will increase transparency, build trust, and ensure that the diverse needs of different employees are met e.g. of national vs international staff. Throughout the process, its important to create a psychologically safe environment that allows employees to speak freely about their needs and concerns.

Make real changes to address the practical needs of your staff

Use a tried and tested tools like Security Risk Management (SRM) Strategy and Policy Development: A Cross-Functional Guide developed by the Global Interagency Security Forum (GISF) and International Location Safety (ILS) to incorporate the learnings from the audit into your organizational strategy and policy.

While the specific policies and safety mechanisms will look different for every organization, the development process should include the following components:

1.        Embed the SRM strategy within the larger organizational strategies to ensure its effectiveness and sustainability.

2.        Ensure that the roles and responsibilities with regard to safety are clear. Consider using an existing framework like the RACI chart.

3.        Integrate the SRM strategy across departments and programs such as finance, IT, and HR. An effective strategy requires the participation of all employees.

4.        Implement a Monitoring, Evaluation, Accountability and Learning (MEAL) plan to measure the SRM strategys impact and improve it over time.

Prioritize workplace psychological safety

Why it matters

Psychological safety is the shared belief among team members that one can take risks, speak openly and plainly, and share ideas, questions, and concerns without judgment or ridicule. When people feel psychologically safe, they are more engaged, less stressed, and overall healthier.

A psychologically safe environment allows people to take risks, which improves innovation. Critical to aid work, it makes it easier to challenge norms and safer to report mistakes and adverse events, leading to learning and organizational growth.

At the organizational level, psychological safety improves productivity, retention, and innovation.

Recommendations

There are many ways to create a psychologically safe workplace. While it comes naturally to some leaders and teams, others have to employ a more intentional approach. Leadership attributes like accessibility, humility, and empathy contribute to a safe environment, as do many of the components outlined in this guide, such as cultivating trusted relationships, offering flexible working conditions, and offering pathways for reciprocal feedback.

There are myriad resources on psychological safety, from free guides to paid workshops. Use the tools that are right for your leadership and managers to develop the skills to create a psychologically safe work environment. Here are some starting points:

Stay in touch with your employees

Promote interpersonal relationships and build an open and inclusive workplace culture. Recognize and reward employees for taking risks, offering new ideas, and learning from failures, reinforcing a culture of innovation and continuous improvement

Establish clear norms and expectations

Make it commonplace to share outside-the-box ideas, to respectfully provide and respond to feedback, and to admit mistakes. Hold leadership and management to the same standards as rank-and-file employees.

Embed psychological safety in the organization

Integrate psychological safety metrics into performance reviews. Hold workshops and trainings for managers and staff. Invest in DEIA initiatives to ensure nobody is excluded.
