Prioritize workplace psychological safety

Why it matters

Psychological safety is the shared belief among team members that one can take risks, speak openly and plainly, and share ideas, questions, and concerns without judgment or ridicule. When people feel psychologically safe, they are more engaged, less stressed, and overall healthier.

A psychologically safe environment allows people to take risks, which improves innovation. Critical to aid work, it makes it easier to challenge norms and safer to report mistakes and adverse events, leading to learning and organizational growth.

At the organizational level, psychological safety improves productivity, retention, and innovation.

Recommendations

There are many ways to create a psychologically safe workplace. While it comes naturally to some leaders and teams, others have to employ a more intentional approach. Leadership attributes like accessibility, humility, and empathy contribute to a safe environment, as do many of the components outlined in this guide, such as cultivating trusted relationships, offering flexible working conditions, and offering pathways for reciprocal feedback.

There are myriad resources on psychological safety, from free guides to paid workshops. Use the tools that are right for your leadership and managers to develop the skills to create a psychologically safe work environment. Here are some starting points:

Stay in touch with your employees

Promote interpersonal relationships and build an open and inclusive workplace culture. Recognize and reward employees for taking risks, offering new ideas, and learning from failures, reinforcing a culture of innovation and continuous improvement

Establish clear norms and expectations

Make it commonplace to share outside-the-box ideas, to respectfully provide and respond to feedback, and to admit mistakes. Hold leadership and management to the same standards as rank-and-file employees.

Embed psychological safety in the organization

Integrate psychological safety metrics into performance reviews. Hold workshops and trainings for managers and staff. Invest in DEIA initiatives to ensure nobody is excluded.

Enable adequate rest

Why it matters

Ensuring employees have adequate rest is crucial for both their physical and mental well-being. Insufficient rest has been found to lead to worse health, decreased performance, higher likelihood of absenteeism, and higher healthcare costs.

Aid organizations working in challenging environments have to be creative to ensure their employees have the opportunity to recover from the demands of work. Normalize and support mental health

Why it matters

Mental ill-health and acute mental illness are unfortunately prevalent among aid organizations, where experiences of trauma and chronic stress are common and risk of PTSD, depression and anxiety is elevated compared to among the general population. Every aspect of the Workplace Wellbeing Assessment relates back to mental health and wellbeing, but this section hones in on preventing, combating, or even living with mental health conditions, as well as addressing the associated stigma.

Like an infection, untreated mental health concerns can grow into complex and dangerous illness. At the very least, this can reduce productivity and effectiveness. In the worst case, untreated mental illness can lead someone to take their own life.

Therefore, it is crucial for organizations to make mental health support readily available and to combat stigma, normalizing discussions of mental health, encouraging employees to seek help when they need it, and creating a psychologically safe environment that discourages employees from repressing or hiding mental health conditions.

Recommendations

Ensure access to mental health resources

Provide free or subsidized mental health resources to all employees via benefits packages, including access to an in-house or independent counselor. Create fact sheets or guides for supervisors and employees to provide quick information on mental health services.

Combat the stigma of seeking help

Encourage open discussions about mental health through awareness campaigns, workshops, and peer support programs. Fostering open dialogue about mental health reduces stigma and increases help-seeking behavior.

While all employees should feel comfortable bringing up mental health concerns, personal and cultural factors may prevent some people from doing so. Therefore, it is important to also provide routes for employees to seek support anonymously, such as anonymous chats.

Protect employees with mental health conditions

In many countries, mental illness is protected under disability laws. Nevertheless, let employees know that being diagnosed does not have to affect their job. Incorporate language into organizational policies to guarantee reasonable accommodations for mental health conditions.

Integrate mental health into the organizational policies

Create and promote Employee Assistance Programs to provide critical services.

Hold annual trainings on peer support methods and recognizing suicide warning signs

Why it matters 

In aid organizations, it is easy to assume that all employees are driven by the mission. Many employees are directly connected to the mission â providing services to beneficiaries and seeing the outcomes of their work on a daily basis â while others, such as administrative staff, are more removed. A strong connection between individual work and an organizationâs mission fosters motivation, engagement, and a sense of purpose. Employees who understand how their contributions align with broader organizational goals experience greater job satisfaction and commitment.

Shared purpose within an organization also enhances collaboration across teams. When employees see how different roles contribute to a collective objective, silos break down, and cooperation improves.

 

Recommendations

Create or review your mission statement

Do you have a well-crafted mission statement, honed over years of meetings, discussions, and consultations? Or have you not really gotten around to creating one yet? Either way, when you create or review the statement, make sure it reflects the current goals and values of the organization and is cognizant of the current state of the world.

Communicate the mission clearly and consistently

Once you have a mission statement make sure that your employees know it, internalize it, and practice it. One way to do this is to flesh it out and make it actionable by creating a Values and Behaviors framework.

To reinforce the mission, values, and behaviors, make them omnipresent throughout the organization:

-          Add them to documents, email signatures, posters.

-          Keep them top of mind by framing strategic meetings and program planning activities around them â ensuring that the organizationâs actions are in line with its mission.

-          Include them in performance reviews to measure how each employee is contributing to them.

-          Integrate them into onboarding and training programs.

-          Celebrate mission-related achievements casually or formally

Share mission-related achievements

While many frontline workers see the how their work contributes to the mission directly, admin staff and others who are more removed from the programs may lack that critical feedback. Sharing success stories from the field, testimonials from beneficiaries, or reports on program outcomes can help employees realize how their work connects to the mission. Whenever possible, take time to highlight how logistical, administrative, or technical roles contribute to life-changing interventions to help all staff members feel connected to the mission.
