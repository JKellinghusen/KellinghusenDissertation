Provide a living wage

Why it matters

A living wage is essential for ensuring economic stability, reducing financial stress, and supporting the overall well-being of employees. When wages do not meet employees’ needs, they are more stressed, more likely to look for better paying work, and more likely to spend their free time earning additional income, rather than resting, decreasing their productivity.

Compensation is also a key determinant of mental health, with financial insecurity being linked to increased anxiety and depression, particularly among workers in lower wage brackets. Providing a living wage ensures that employees can meet their basic needs, leading to improved workplace morale, stronger performance, and greater employee retention.

Considering these factors, it is usually a better financial decision to raise wages – especially for the lowest-paid workers – rather than suffering from low productivity and high turnover.

Recommendations

Align wages with local cost of living

Assess and adjust salaries based on local cost-of-living data rather than relying solely on industry benchmarks. Conduct periodic wage reviews to ensure that employees can afford basic necessities without financial hardship. Keep in mind that a decent living wage is often higher than the government-mandated minimum wage.

Adjust wages based on experience and skill development

Tying raises to employee experience, skills, and tenure encourages long-term retention and professional growth. Make salary progression transparent and linked to clearly defined career pathways, helping employees see a future within the organization rather than seeking higher pay elsewhere.

Offer financial wellness support

Beyond wages, organizations can improve employees' financial security by offering benefits such as retirement plans, financial coaching, and debt management resources, which can reduce employee stress and improve overall job performance.

Ensure wage equity across all employee categories

Contract workers, national staff, and international employees should all receive fair and equitable compensation. In many humanitarian organizations, disparities exist between different categories of workers, leading to dissatisfaction and high turnover rates among local staff. While paying everyone the same is neither feasible nor expected, organizations can intentionally structure pay to provide all employees sufficient resources to succeed in life, and make this structure transparent to all staff.
